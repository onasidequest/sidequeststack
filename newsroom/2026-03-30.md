# Newsroom Update — March 28–30, 2026

## 1. Apple Intelligence Accidentally Launched in China, Then Got Pulled
Apple's AI features briefly appeared on iPhones in China on March 30 — except they weren't supposed to. China requires Apple to partner with local companies like Alibaba to power any AI features before they're allowed to roll out. Apple apparently jumped the gun, pulled the features, and issued a quiet "that was an error" statement. The irony of an AI feature launching in error is not lost on me. In related news: Apple is also quietly building out an "AI App Store" inside the Siri Extensions framework coming in iOS 27, which would let users install third-party AI chatbots the same way they install apps today. Google Gemini, ChatGPT, and whoever else makes the cut would be available as drop-in Siri replacements. For creators and developers, that's a real unlock — your AI tool could live inside every iPhone.

## 2. Microsoft Copilot Cowork Now Mixes GPT and Claude in the Same Task
Microsoft started rolling out a feature called Copilot Cowork through its Frontier Program that's genuinely interesting: it lets Claude (from Anthropic) give an "accuracy edit pass" on research that GPT drafted first. Researcher + Critique, as they're calling it — one model writes, the other checks. They also added support for "long-running, multi-step tasks." Whether this is great AI engineering or two AI companies charging Microsoft double is left as an exercise to the reader. Either way, it's moving toward agents that loop on each other, which is the real direction this is all heading.

## 3. The Music Industry Has a "Don't Ask, Don't Tell" Policy on AI
Rolling Stone has a deep piece out this week on how AI music has quietly taken over huge parts of hip-hop production. Producer Young Guru estimates more than half of sample-based hip-hop is now using AI-generated funk and soul rather than licensing originals or hiring session musicians. Artists across genres are using it to demo arrangements, experiment with sounds, and build template material. Nobody wants to admit it, though. The official stance from most artists is silence — which, as the article notes, is a strange kind of open secret. For anyone in the digital product or content space, this is a useful data point: AI tools have crossed the "embarrassing to admit" threshold in a lot of industries before anyone said anything out loud.
