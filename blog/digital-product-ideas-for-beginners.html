25 Digital Product Ideas for Beginners: Ranked by Difficulty and Demand

Find your starting point — then actually start

By Buster · Side Quest Stack · March 2026

Every list of digital product ideas on the internet includes "ebooks" and "printables" with no further context. That's not useful. What you actually need to know is: how hard is this to make, does anyone actually buy it, and can a beginner without specialized skills produce something sellable?

I've ranked these 25 ideas on two dimensions: difficulty (how hard to create) and demand (how active the market is). Easy + high demand is where you should start. Hard + low demand is where you should not start, regardless of how interesting the idea sounds.

Difficulty scale: Easy = no specialized skills, Canva or basic tools sufficient. Medium = some skill or learning curve required. Hard = significant expertise or time investment.

Tier 1: Easy to Make, High Demand (Start Here)

  1. Planner pages and templates — Easy
    Daily, weekly, monthly planners. Bullet journal layouts. Academic planners. The market is enormous and buyers are habitual — someone who buys one planner often buys several. Canva handles this entirely.
  2. Checklists — Easy
    Moving checklists, cleaning checklists, packing lists, wedding checklists. Simple to design, high search volume, evergreen demand. A well-made PDF checklist can sell for years.
  3. Social media templates (Canva) — Easy
    Instagram story templates, Pinterest pin templates, LinkedIn post graphics. Huge market among small business owners who can't design. Sell as editable Canva link files.
  4. Budget trackers and finance spreadsheets — Easy
    Google Sheets or Excel templates for tracking spending, savings goals, debt payoff. High intent buyers (people who want to fix their finances are motivated to buy tools).
  5. Wall art / printable art — Easy
    Typography prints, botanical illustrations, inspirational quotes. Low price point ($2–5), high volume market. You can generate many variations from one design.
  6. Kids' activity sheets — Easy
    Coloring pages, handwriting practice, math worksheets, alphabet sheets. Parents are motivated buyers, especially for homeschool families. Seasonal themes sell reliably.
  7. Resume and CV templates — Easy
    Clean, professional Word or Google Doc templates. High demand (people job hunt constantly), low difficulty if you have good design taste. Priced $5–15.

Tier 2: Medium Difficulty, High Demand

  1. Notion templates — Medium
    Notion has a huge and growing user base who buy templates constantly. Requires knowing Notion well, but if you use it yourself, you're already halfway there. Strong price point ($10–25+).
  2. Email templates and scripts — Medium
    Business email templates for follow-ups, proposals, cold outreach. High value to buyers (saves them time writing). Requires good writing skills, not design skills.
  3. Mini ebooks and guides (under 30 pages) — Medium
    Focused how-to guides on a specific skill or topic. The mistake beginners make is trying to write a 200-page book. A 15-page "Complete Guide to [Specific Thing]" is more valuable and faster to produce.
  4. Recipe collections — Medium
    Themed recipe ebooks (10-minute dinners, air fryer recipes, specific diet types). If you cook, you already have the expertise. Layout in Canva. Market is enormous.
  5. Business card templates — Medium
    Editable business card designs. Buyers customize their info and take it to a local printer. Low price point, very high volume.
  6. Habit trackers (digital and print) — Medium
    Combined print + digital products work well here. GoodNotes and Notability templates for iPad users. Growing market as productivity culture expands.
  7. Wedding stationery templates — Medium
    Invitation suites, seating chart templates, table number designs. High price point ($10–40), high intent buyers, seasonal demand spikes predictably. Competition is real but so is the market.
  8. Canva brand kit templates — Medium
    Full brand kit packages: logo, color palette, fonts, social templates. Small business owners pay real money for this. Priced $15–50. Requires design eye.

Tier 3: Medium Difficulty, Moderate Demand

  1. Lightroom presets — Medium
    Photo editing presets for photographers. Requires Adobe Lightroom (or free alternatives) and an eye for photo editing. Niche but passionate buyer base.
  2. PowerPoint / Keynote presentation templates — Medium
    Professional slide decks for business presentations. High value to corporate buyers. Less Etsy, more Gumroad or direct sales.
  3. Meal plan templates — Medium
    Weekly meal planners with grocery list integration. Works as PDF or spreadsheet. Health and wellness is a perpetually strong niche.
  4. Contract and legal templates — Hard
    Freelancer contracts, client agreements. High price point ($20–100+) and strong demand from freelancers. Requires real expertise — consult an attorney or source properly. Significant liability if wrong.
  5. Fonts and brush packs — Hard
    Custom typefaces or Procreate brush packs. Extremely high demand but requires either design expertise or specialized skills. Earns royalties for years if good.

Tier 4: High Difficulty (Build Up To These)

  1. Online courses (video) — Hard
    The highest earning digital product category. Also the hardest: requires expertise, recording equipment, editing skills, and a platform. Don't start here. Build an audience first.
  2. Stock photography packs — Hard
    High quality photos for bloggers and small businesses to use. Requires photography skills and equipment. Very competitive market.
  3. SVG cut files (for Cricut) — Hard
    Vector files for cutting machines. Passionate niche with real purchase intent. Requires vector design skills (Illustrator or Inkscape).
  4. Procreate illustration packs — Hard
    Digital illustrations for commercial use. Only viable if you can draw. High ceiling on earnings if you can.
  5. Software tools and scripts — Hard
    Scripts, plugins, automations. Requires programming skills. High price point, loyal niche buyers, no direct competitors if your tool is genuinely useful.

Where We Started and Why

Disco Dazzler Studio started with Tier 1 products — printable planners, templates, and activity sheets. Simple to produce, real market demand, and the feedback loop is fast. We've learned what buyers actually respond to before investing time in more complex products.

Our advice: pick one idea from Tier 1, make five variations of it, list them, and see what happens. Market feedback is more valuable than planning. Make something, put it in front of buyers, then iterate based on what they do.
