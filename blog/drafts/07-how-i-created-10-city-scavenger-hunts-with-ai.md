# How I Created 10 City Scavenger Hunts with AI (And What I Learned About Etsy SEO)

When I tell people I created ten city scavenger hunt PDFs in a few days, they usually picture me leisurely sipping something and clicking "generate." They're not entirely wrong, but they're also picturing about 5% of the actual process. The other 95% was layout problems, keyword research rabbit holes, and one afternoon where I spent three hours making sure the clue font was readable on both A4 and US Letter paper sizes simultaneously.

Let me walk you through all of it.

---

## The Idea

City scavenger hunts are a specific kind of product I had been thinking about for a while. They're printable PDFs people download for date nights, bachelorette parties, family trips, team-building days. The buyer takes it to a city, follows the clues, checks off landmarks. Low cost to make. Real use case. The kind of thing someone might actually buy.

The SEO data backed this up. When I ran keywords through eRank — which is a third-party Etsy analytics tool that shows you search volume and competition — I found that terms like "city scavenger hunt printable" and "date night scavenger hunt" had meaningful search volume with manageable competition. Not "zero competition because nobody wants this" and not "ten thousand shops all selling identical versions." There was a window.

The plan was to make ten of them, covering cities where this kind of product made intuitive sense: places people visit specifically to walk around, see things, take photos. New York. San Francisco. Chicago. New Orleans. That kind of geography.

---

## How AI Actually Helped (And Where It Didn't)

Let's be specific, because "I used AI to make this" can mean anything from "I asked ChatGPT to write one sentence" to "an AI designed, wrote, and formatted everything start to finish." I want to be honest about what actually happened.

The AI part that worked really well: writing the clues themselves. Each scavenger hunt needed twenty-something clues per city, calibrated to a mix of difficulty, arranged in a logical walking order, and written in a tone that was playful without being irritating. That's a lot of content per product, and generating a solid first draft of clues for ten cities manually would have taken significantly longer. I'd give the AI a city, a list of landmarks, a target audience (couples, families, etc.), and a tone brief, and it would return something I could actually edit.

The AI part that required more work than expected: making those clues accurate. AI will confidently give you clue directions that involve a building that was demolished in 2019, or describe a mural that exists on a different street than stated. Every single clue needed a verification pass. I'm not complaining — this is just the reality of using language models for location-specific content. The draft speed was valuable; the accuracy pass was unavoidable.

The part AI could not help with at all: layout.

---

## The PDF Layout Problem Nobody Talks About

I'm going to tell you about the PDF layout phase because I think people who write about Etsy digital products skip over this entirely and it makes the whole thing sound easier than it is.

A well-formatted printable PDF is not just text on a page. It needs to:

- Look good when printed at home on a basic inkjet
- Not waste ink by covering everything in dark backgrounds
- Work at both US Letter (8.5 x 11) and A4 (slightly narrower, slightly taller) since buyers are in different countries
- Have enough visual interest that it looks like a product, not a Word document
- Be readable under variable outdoor lighting conditions, since people actually use these things outside

This meant a lot of iteration in Canva. I had a design I liked, then discovered it bled past the safe margins at A4. Fixed that, then the title header got visually cramped. Fixed that, then the clue boxes were too small on mobile preview. This sounds tedious because it is tedious. It's also the work that separates a product that looks finished from one that looks like it was assembled in an afternoon.

The watercolor illustration elements helped here. I used a set of loose, hand-painted-style watercolor illustrations — compass roses, map elements, landmark silhouettes — to give each hunt its own visual identity without turning the whole thing into a black ink nightmare. They also softened the grid-heavy layout that scavenger hunt formats naturally produce. Functionally, it made each product look cohesive and slightly premium at a glance.

Whether buyers actually care about watercolor elements in a scavenger hunt PDF is a different question I don't have a definitive answer to yet. Anecdotally: they look good in listing photos, which matters more than the actual PDF quality for initial conversion.

## The eRank Audit

Before listing anything, I ran an eRank audit on the shop and on the specific keywords I was planning to target.

A few things I learned that were genuinely useful:

**Tag specificity matters more than I thought.** Etsy gives you 13 tags per listing. I was writing tags like "printable" and "digital download" — which are technically accurate but so broad they're useless for discovery. eRank's competition data showed me I was essentially tagging into categories where I had no realistic chance of appearing. The better approach was long-tail: "San Francisco scavenger hunt printable" and "outdoor date night activity printable" instead of "printable" alone.

**Titles are more important than descriptions for Etsy search.** I knew this in theory. The eRank data made it viscerally clear. The first 40 characters of your title carry the most weight, and I had been leading with product names rather than search terms. A title like "City Scavenger Hunt PDF — San Francisco" works less well than "San Francisco Scavenger Hunt Printable — Date Night Adventure Game for Couples." Same product, different likelihood of appearing in search.

**Renewal gaming is real but limited.** Some sellers renew listings constantly to get fresh-listing visibility boosts. eRank data suggests this works for a short window and then decays. I'm not doing this aggressively, but it's worth knowing.

**Your listing photos are your conversion, not your discovery.** This one wasn't from eRank specifically, but it's what I kept coming back to. eRank can help you get found. Once someone clicks, you're selling them with images. Getting the right mockup photos — showing the PDF in context, on a phone, printed out, being used on a city street — matters at least as much as SEO once someone is on your listing.

---

## What I'd Do Differently

I would start the eRank research before creating the products, not after. I did it in roughly parallel — running research while also building — which meant I had to go back and adjust titles and tags on products I'd already made. Starting with the keyword data first would have let me build the listings with better metadata from day one.

I would also spend more time on listing photos before launch rather than treating them as something to improve later. The first impression a listing makes is almost entirely visual, and I knew this going in, and I still shipped some photos I wasn't fully happy with because I wanted to get the products live. Classic mistake.

The AI draft-to-edit workflow is genuinely good. I would do that the same way again. Generate fast, verify carefully, don't trust location details.

---

## Where Things Stand

Ten hunts are listed. The SEO is better than my first Etsy listings, which is a low bar but still a bar. The watercolor layouts look like products rather than homework assignments.

I will report back on whether any of this translates to actual sales. That part is genuinely unknown. The Etsy algorithm is a black box, word-of-mouth doesn't exist yet for a shop with no reviews, and I am fighting for visibility against shops that have been running for years.

But the products are real and they're listed and that's more than I had before I started.

That counts for something. Probably.
